A hash table is a data structure that maps unique keys to indexes in an array of buckets using a hash function. Hash tables provide, on average, O(1) time performance for searches, insertions, and deletions. In other words, the time required to search for, insert, or delete a value from the data structure is constant regardless of the amount of data stored. A hash function is a function that maps keys to a unique index in the array where the data can be stored or looked for. Hash functions need to be:
- Quick, otherwise it defeats the purpose of the hash table.
- Collision-resistant, meaning different keys will rarely, if ever, generate the same index.
- Uniform in their outputs, meaning each index is equally likely to be chosen by the hash function.

Insertion is as easy as running a key through the hash function and storing the value at that index, which is a constant time operation. Search is the same, although instead of storing the object, you calculate the hash and retrieve the object at that index, which is a constant time operation. Deletion is the same. Effectively, you trade the linear complexity of scanning with the speed of an array lookup. The catch here is that it costs extra space. A hash table demonstrates the space-time tradeoff of algorithms - we trade better time complexity for increased linear space complexity.

No hash function is perfect, so hash tables need collision-handling to deal with cases where it does occur. Two options here are chaining and open addressing.

Chaining creates stores a linked list at each index, with each key-value pair at that index having its own node. Items mapped to this index are stored by adding them to the linked list, which is a constant time operation. Searching is a little more complicated because it requires traversing the linked list, which is normally a linear time operation. However, with proper design, we can minimize this effect and amortize the cost of the search, keeping search on average constant. Deletion is similar to insertion, since removal from a linked list is a constant time operation. Since the hash function ideally distributes indices equally, chaining will keep things efficient for a while.

The other alternative is open addressing. With open addressing, every index stores a key-value pair normally. In the event of a collision, the hash table will use a probing sequence to find the first free space and insert there instead.

Both these solutions are vulnerable to the ratio of stored objects to table capacity, called the load factor. As the size of the table increases, the load factor increases, increasing the probability of collision. The performance of the hash table slowly degrades as the load factor increases, trending away from constant time and towards linear. To fix this, once the load factor reaches a certain point, many hash table implementations implement mandatory resizing, doubling the size of the table, rehashing everything, and continuing. This is a linear time operation, but math of doubling each time makes it less and less frequent as the size of the table increases. In chaining implementations, the load factor measures the average length of each linked list, meaning as it increases, the average list gets bigger and bigger and searches become more and more expensive. With open addressing, higher load factors means each probing sequence has to spend more time looking for an open spot.

Hash tables should be used in situations fast lookups, insertions, and deletions are needed. They're useful in LeetCode problems for searching over linear scans. Good for detecting duplicates or counting frequencies. Avoid them if order matters.

Speed matters: Why working quickly is more important than it seems
- Working quickly is obviously important because you complete more. However, it also lowers the cost of doing things, inclining you to do more.c
- The inverse is true. Every time you take longer to do something, you train your brain that it takes long, discouraging you from doing it more.
- Even worse, doing things slow means you'll do it slow in the future because you don't learn to complete things quickly through repetition.
- Any to-do list not emptied fast enough becomes a to-do list that is either never emptied or abandoned.
- Broadly speaking, systems that consume quickly get more to consume. Slow systems starve.
- Part of the activation energy required for a task is the image it evokes in your head. If the image it evokes is slow, then that energy feels higher.
- Prescription: If there's something you want to do, do more, and get good at -- writing, fixing bugs, exercising - you should try to do it faster.
- Don't be sloppy, but push yourself to go faster. You'll be inclined to do it more, and as you do it more with deliberate practice, you'll get both fast and good.
- Being fast is fun. You begin to feel capable of doing the things you've always wanted to do. It makes you feel agentic.

Vigils:
Review session logs from previous day. Identify entries eligible for promotion to a working document. Update errata logs with failures identified during previous day's Compline. No AI consultation.

Lauds:
First period of the working day. Write session brief for the day's primary inquiry. No AI interface open. Session brief should state (a) the problem in precise terms, (b) what a satisfactory output looks like, (c) which verification method applies to output type, and (d) what constitutes failure. Session brief logged before Terce.

Problem (a):
I want to understand hash tables/hash maps. What is a hash table? How do they work? What's their time and space complexity? When should you use one? How are they implemented in languages like Python and the C programming language? What is an associative array abstract data structure? What are its mathematical properties?

Problem (b):
Satisfactory output would be answers to the above questions that are clear and understandable, while also being technically accurate. It would also include a code implementation in Python and the C programming language.

Problem (c):
Verification of the output type means checking with references like CLRS and other authoritative texts in data structures and algorithms. The code should pass test cases, match the complexity nature expressed in the original definition, and be referenced against existing implementations that likely exist on the Internet.

Problem (d):
Failure would be either inaccurate information about the nature of the data structure/abstract data type, or the code failing in specific test cases.

Terce
Primary AI consultation session. The practitioner engages with the AI using the session brief as the governing document. All outputs are logged to the session log immediately. Nothing produced in Terce is treated as verified. Deviation from the session brief is permitted if the practitioner pauses, writes an amendment to the session brief stating the deviation and its rationale, and logs it before continuing.

Sext
Verification session. The practitioner works through Terce outputs against the verification standards specified for the relevant domain. Verified content is promoted to working document
status. Failures are logged to the errata corpus with the required fields. The ratio of verified content to logged failures from each Sext session is the organization's primary quality metric.

None
Integration session. The practitioner places verified outputs in the context of the existing corpus. Identifies connections to prior verified results. Identifies open problems the day's verified work reveals. Updates domain protocols if Terce and Sext revealed that existing protocols were insufficient. Protocol updates are working documents, reviewed again the following
Vigils before being treated as settled.

Vespers
Synthesis and publication session. The practitioner converts integration work into publishable corpus entries where the material meets the publication standard. Drafts any public-facing writing. Only corpus-grade material may be published externally. The practitioner does not publish session log or working document material under any circumstance, regardless
of how interesting it appears.

Compline
Error review. The practitioner reviews the day's errata corpus entries. For each failure: identifies the failure type, determines whether the verification protocol caught it as designed or
whether it was caught by other means, and proposes a protocol amendment if the protocol missed it. Reviews the day's session brief against what actually occurred in Terce. Documents deviations and their causes.

What do I know about Mars?
Mars is the fourth planet from the Sun, directly after Earth. This traditionally comes from the notion of the order of the planets by distance. However, this isn't always true, as there are times where Mars is closer to the Sun than the Earth is due to different points in their orbits. Together with Earth, Venus, and Mercury, Mars is one of the rocky terrestrial planets, in contrast to the large gas giants like Jupiter, Saturn, Neptune, and Uranus. It seems like the only two places in a star's orbit that rocky planets can occur is reasonably close to it, and far away from it in the icy recesses of space. Mars is predominantly red, due to the high amounts of iron oxide in its crust. The Martian regolith is toxic to plants due to perchlorate in the dirt, and its atmosphere is mostly carbon dioxide. Mars can be thought of as a desert planet, with little water except the tiny bit in its atmosphere and buried under its surface. The time to travel from Earth to Mars is about 6 months by normal human spaceflight. Some major landmarks on the surface of Mars are Olympus Mons, the highest mountain peak in the solar system, and Valles Marineris - a massive canyon that puts the Grand Canyon on the surface of Earth to shame. An open question in astronomy is whether Mars currently supports, or one point ever supported, life. It's possible Mars had large amounts of water in its past, but lost it due to events in space.
