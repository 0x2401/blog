Molecular Biology of the Cell
- Earth has life - organisms that consume energy to maintain their internal organization and make copies of themselves, passing down characteristics via genetic information in a process called heredity. This is one of the things that makes life unique compared to other processes like growing crystals. All living things are made out of cells.
- Every complex lifeform originated from one cell, meaning that one cell held all of the genetic information needed for the organism, and duplicated into other cells holding the same complete copy of information.
- Furthermore, all life on Earth has similar biochemistry and can process and understand each others' genetic code, implying that every form of life on Earth inherited this system from a common ancestor billions of years ago.
- The molecule that stores genetic information is called deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA). On a molecular level, DNA is two strands of sugar-phosphate polymer backbone with a sequence of four possible nucleotide bases - adenine (A), guanine (G), cytosine (C), and thymine (T). These two strands are coiled around each other in a double helix. Each nucleotide on one strand always pairs with an opposite nucleotide on the other strand - adenine with thymine and guanine with cytosine. This means that having one strand of a DNA molecule gives enough information to construct its opposite strand.
- This is how cells make copies of their genetic information via a process called template polymerization. One DNA strand is used as a template to create a complementary DNA strand. This process is performed on both strands of a DNA molecule, creating four strands in total - two new and two template. By pairing them together, you get two new complete DNA molecules that are exact copies of the original.
- Cells can't just store their genetic information. It actually needs to be used for something. That something is the synthesis of proteins, which are biomolecules that perform some important function within an organism. The instructions for creating these proteins are encoded in the sequence of As, Gs, Cs, and Ts that make up the genetic code.
- That sequence is used to create proteins via transcription and translation. Along with DNA, there is a similar single-stranded molecule called RNA, or ribonucleic acid. RNA has the same nucleotide sequence as DNA, with the exception of thymine replaced by uracil, so the potential letters are AGCU rather than AGCT. Transcription opens up the DNA molecule and creates a complementary RNA molecule off one of the strands; if the DNA molecule strand sequence is ACGAATCCGGT, the RNA molecule created will be UGCUUAGGCCA. The information of the DNA is copied or *transcribed* into a disposable information molecule that can be used for the next step of the process.
- During translation, the newly-transcribed RNA molecule pairs with a part of the cell called a ribosome to construct the actual protein based on the instructions encoded.
